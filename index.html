12' · BUILDING PLAY

Where It Comes From

Our Kick Shot board game is inspired by several popular games. We borrowed the dice-based movement from Monopoly, where the number rolled determines how far a player can move. The action card system was inspired by games like UNO and Mario Party, where special cards or events can change the course of the game and add unpredictability. We also took inspiration from the teamwork and passing mechanics found in the video game EA Sports FC 25 (formerly FIFA), incorporating realistic soccer rules such as yellow cards, red cards, free kicks, injuries, and shooting opportunities. By combining these mechanics with our own board game design, we created a game that captures the excitement and strategy of a World Cup soccer match while remaining fun and accessible for players of all ages.

Official Rules

1Game Overview

Mini Soccer is a strategy-based board game that combines movement, shooting, and special card abilities. Teams compete to score the most goals by moving strategically across the board, using cards, and taking successful shots.

2Players & Teams

Each team consists of 2 players. The game can be played with:

  • 2 teams (1 vs 1)
  • 4 teams (free-for-all competition)

Each team controls:

  • 1 soccer ball
  • 2 player pieces
  • 10 action cards

3Board Setup

Before starting the game:

2-Team Game

  • Place 2 goals on opposite sides of the board.
  • Each team defends one goal and attacks the opponent's goal.

4-Team Game

  • Place 4 goals, one in each corner of the board.
  • Each team is assigned a goal to defend.

Starting Setup

  • Place all player pieces infront of their nets in a row.
  • Shuffle Cards and handout 10 cards to each team.
  • Place the soccer ball at the center of the grid.

4Starting the Game

  • A coin toss determines which team starts with possession of the ball.
  • The starting team begins the first turn.
  • Turns continue clockwise around the board.

5Turn Sequence

During each team's turn:

Step 1 — Choose Active Player

  • One player from the active team becomes the Attacker.
  • The other teammate remains on the board as a Defender / Support Player and may help with future plays.
  • On their following turn the Defender becomes the Attacker and the Attacker becomes the Defender and vice versa

Step 2 — Roll and Move

  • The Attacker rolls the die.
  • Move the player piece the exact number of spaces shown on the die.

6Shooting Rules

After moving, check the player's location. If the player lands in a Shooting Zone (the colored part infront the net):

  • The player becomes the attacker; the opposing team's player acts as the defender.
  • The attacker will attempt a shot by flicking the mini soccer ball toward the goal.
  • If the ball enters the goal, the attacking team earns 1 point.
  • If the shot misses, no points are awarded.

After the shot, the ball is given to the opposing team and the next team begins their turn.

7Card Rules

If the player does not land in a Shooting Zone, they must use one card from their team's 10-card deck.

  • A player may only use one card per turn.
  • The card effect must be completed immediately.
  • Used cards are placed in a discard pile and cannot be reused.

Possible card effects may include: moving player pieces, blocking opponents, stealing possession, changing movement options, or creating attacking opportunities.

After the card effect, possession passes to the next team.

8Team Roles During Play

Each turn has two roles:

Attacker

  • Rolls the die and moves.
  • Attempts shots when entering a Shooting Zone.
  • Uses cards when not in a Shooting Zone.

Defender / Support Player

  • The teammate who does not move during the current turn.
  • Remains on the board.
  • Helps defend against opponents and prepares for future turns.

Players will switch roles between turns.

9Possession Rules

  • After every shot attempt (goal or miss), possession goes to the opposing team.
  • After every completed card action, possession goes to the next team.
  • A team cannot take another turn until all other teams have completed their turns.

10End of Game

The game continues clockwise until every team has used all 10 of their cards. Once all cards have been used:

  • Count each team's total goals.
  • Each successful goal is worth 1 point.
  • The team with the highest score wins.

11Tie-Breaker — Penalty Kicks

If two or more teams have the same score:

  • Each tied team takes one final penalty shot.
  • Teams take turns attempting shots.
  • The first team to score while the others miss is declared the winner.

12Winning the Game

🏆 The winner is the team with the most goals after all cards are used, or the first team to score during sudden-death penalty kicks.

58' · ON THE PITCH

Playtesting Log

1Dexterity & The "Shooting Zone"

Focus: Evaluating the flicking mechanic and scoring rates.

Observation: Originally, players were allowed to attempt a flick shot from anywhere on the board. This resulted in messy gameplay where pieces can be knocked over by shots frequently, and very low scoring rates.

Insight: The dexterity element (flicking) is fun but needs constraints to feel like a tactical soccer play.

Revision Implemented: We introduced the Shooting Zone. Players can now only attempt a shot if their dice roll lands them within the shooting zone. This created a much better tactical environment where defenders now know exactly where they need to block, and attackers must carefully calculate their moves to land in the zone.

2Player Engagement & Downtime

Focus: Testing the 4-team (free-for-all) mode and overall turn pacing.

Observation: In a 4-team game, once a team finished their turn, they would mentally check out until their turn. Moreover, the second player on each team felt useless until they were chosen as the "Active Player."

Insight: We needed to improve engagement.

Revision Implemented: We reformatted the role of the teammate into a Defender/Support Role — while they don't roll the die, they remain on the board as a physical obstacle to block opponents' movement and shooting lanes. We also introduced Active Defense: when an attacker enters a Shooting Zone, the opposing team's player officially acts as the defender. This forces the defending team to stay highly engaged during the attacker's turn, as they must physically participate in the shot-blocking phase.

3Game Length & Pacing

Focus: Finding the right win conditions and preventing endless matches.

Observation: We initially tested a "first to 5 goals wins" condition. In games where players shoot really badly, progress is really slow.

Insight: The game lacked a definitive climax. We needed a timer or something that could escalate tension.

Revision Implemented: We shifted the game structure to be dictated by the decks. End of Game was rewritten so that the game ends once every team has exhausted their 10 cards. This solved pacing issues by capping the game at exactly 10 rounds per team. To resolve the inevitable close games this created, we added the Sudden-Death Penalty Kicks (Section 11), which provided a highly climactic and exciting finale.

72' · THE STAT SHEET

Quantitative Analysis of Game Balance

1Design Choices

We chose to design and structure our game loosely around the actual rules of soccer to retain a certain familiarity and charm. We also took some liberties when adding the possible option for four teams to better adapt for a board game for group settings and differing player counts. The grid layout of the board was chosen to give a higher level of freedom of movement compared to other board games to better replicate soccer.

2Probabilities

The first and foremost probability affected aspect of the game is the starting coin toss that decides which team begins. Other aspects that involve probability include shuffling of cards and the opportunities that are given from the random cards. The probability of cards is driven by how many of each card there is out of the stack of 10 cards.

Additionally, the dice roll that determines how many squares each player can move is essentially random. Skill of player — specifically as it pertains to flicking the ball to pass or attempt to score on net once the kick/pass card is pulled — can also be grouped in as a probability affected aspect of the game flow.

3Time Duration Analysis

A full complete game that takes advantage of all features we found ideally takes from 20–30 minutes for a full playthrough/experience, which differs from our minimum play time of 15 minutes for two teams — but this time is also affected by number of players, not just teams. We found the total duration scales similarly for four teams.

An average turn takes about 10 seconds between card pull, dice roll, movement, and action, depending on if the player received a card that lets them take an action at the end of their turn.

78' · READING THE GAME

Game Dynamics

A good strategy is to reserve good cards like the green shooting card until pieces are in a good position or in the late game to score a tiebreaker. Positioning defender pieces inside and around the shooting zone is an effective way to block or intercept shots. Players who actively move pieces into unguarded positions to attempt shots in the next round are highly effective.

82' · TOUGH CALLS

Trade-offs & Dilemmas

The main dilemma players can encounter is whether to pass or kick the ball when given the chance with the respective card. There is an inherent trade off when a card is taken every turn as it could be a beneficial card or one that has a negative impact for the player. The skill aspect of flicking the ball into the net can also be seen as something beneficial or a tradeoff depending on how skilled the player is.

Typical Play Session
